The goal
One sentence, measurable

Cut the time from beauty concern to confident cart from 15–20 minutes to under 5, and lift browse-to-buy conversion from ~2% to ~3.5% — by fusing Nykaa's existing, scattered intelligence into one guided routine.

Opportunity · the wedge below
The risk

If a shopper asks ChatGPT or a Google shopping agent "best vitamin C serum for my skin," Nykaa can be cut out of discovery entirely — agents are forecast to mediate roughly half of online shopping by 2030.

How Nykaa stands out

01
Neutral, multi-brand, India-first. L'Oréal's agent sells L'Oréal. Nykaa can be the trusted matchmaker across 200K products and 2,000+ brands — tuned to Indian skin, climate, and budgets.
02
Routine, not product. Rivals match one shade. A guided cross-brand AM/PM routine is a multi-item basket only a broad marketplace can assemble.
03
Omnichannel proof loop. Diagnose on the app → confirm shade in one of 237 stores → the model learns → replenish online. No app-only or offline-only rival can close that loop.
04
India skin-texture data as the moat. Train concern analysis — pigmentation, oiliness, acne — on first-party Indian-skin data that global agents simply don't have.
The real moat

Guided routines aren't novel — Sephora and Tira run guided flows, and a rival could copy the four screens in a quarter. The feature isn't the defensibility. What can't be copied quickly is the connected data underneath it — reviews, scans, purchase history, and in-store confirmation tied to one identity — and a neutrality you can govern and prove. The screens are the demo; the moat is the plumbing.

Sleeper bet — the review corpus

P3 is ranked last on effort, first on moat.

Nykaa sits on millions of reviews across 200K+ products. Today that's raw text a shopper has to wade through. Mined into structured, skin-type-tagged signals — "held up in Chennai humidity," "no breakouts on oily skin," "pilled under sunscreen" — the same corpus stops being homework and becomes infrastructure.

Confidence layer"People with your skin profile chose this," shown at the decision point and inside the swap screen — not buried in a reviews tab.
Ranking inputThe tie-breaker the routine engine needs to choose between fifteen near-identical 4.3★ serums. Reviews become the signal search never used.
Data moatIndia skin-type-tagged outcomes at this scale aren't something a neutral foreign agent — L'Oréal's Beauty Genius — can replicate. Every order widens the gap.

Why it stays P3: turning unstructured reviews into trustworthy, tagged signals is real NLP and quality-control effort. But it's the one lever that compounds — which is why it earns a roadmap line, not a backlog grave.

Why this order

Build My Routine wins P0 not because it's easiest, but because it sits where impact is highest and the underlying data is already strong. Skin Genius is the fast follow — high confidence, low lift. The matrix maps impact against effort; size of pain decides the sequence.

Act 08 — Deep dive
P0

Build My Routine

The P0 lever, in detail. Build My Routine is a single engine that turns one stated concern into a complete, add-to-cart-ready regimen — the same four steps, run every time. How it flexes to each shopper, and keeps adapting, is the next act; here's what happens inside the engine.

Inside the engine — four steps

STEP 01

Tell us your skin

Concern, type, and goals in a few taps — pre-filled from Skin Genius and past purchases when they exist.

→
STEP 02

Get a matched routine

A sequenced AM/PM regimen — cleanse, treat, protect — with the reasoning shown, not hidden.

→
STEP 03

Swap to fit you

Trade any product for alternatives by budget, brand, or finish — the routine recalculates around the change.

→
STEP 04

Add the full routine

One tap sends the whole regimen to cart, with smart replenishment reminders set for each item.

Why it's the wedge: it reuses signals Nykaa already owns, shortens the path for the highest-intent shoppers first, and creates the multi-item, repeat-friendly basket that every downstream metric depends on.

Act 12 — Can we build it?

Most teardowns stop at the mockup. This is where ideas die.

Four engineering realities behind Build My Routine — and an honest size on each lever, so the priority order earns its place instead of asserting it.

Cold start
A brand-new user has no scan and no history.
Start from the three stated inputs — concern, skin type, budget — mapped to a small set of expert-curated starter routines per concern×skin cell, with popularity priors from similar first-time profiles layered on top. Personalization deepens as scans, purchases, and swaps accrue. No cold-start answer means no launch — so this is P0 inside the P0.
Latency
Ranking across 200K SKUs can't add seconds to the page.
Don't rank the catalog live. Precompute candidate sets per (concern × skin × budget) segment offline and cache them; at request time the engine only re-ranks a few dozen candidates against the user's signals and live inventory. Heavy ML offline, light lookup online — the p95 latency budget stays intact.
Data quality
The engine is only as good as the product metadata.
Inconsistent ingredient lists and missing concern tags across 200K SKUs produce garbage routines. This is the real dependency — and the honest reason the review corpus matters: reviews backfill attributes the catalog lacks. But it needs a normalization pass first. Name it as a prerequisite, not a given.
Review NLP
Turning millions of free-text reviews into trustworthy signals is hard.
Claim extraction ("broke me out," "held up in humidity"), skin-type linking, confidence thresholds, and quality control against fake or low-signal reviews — before a single signal reaches a shopper. That effort is exactly why P3 stays high-effort, and exactly why it compounds into a moat once built.

Act 13 — The hard calls

A solution with only upside hasn't been thought through.

Three objections this feature would get in its first review — on priority, on revenue, and on trust. Here's the tension, and where I'd land on each. The point isn't that they're solved; it's that they're not ignored.

01 · Priority
Is discovery even Nykaa's biggest problem — over retention, margin, or quick commerce?
The tension

Retention, margin pressure, and the 10-minute-delivery threat are all fighting for the same roadmap slot. Asserting "discovery is #1" without weighing them is exactly how PMs build the wrong thing well.

Where I land

Discovery earns P0 because it's upstream of the others — a shopper who can't decide never becomes a retained, higher-margin, repeat customer. Fix the front door and the retention levers have someone to act on. But I'd hold it loosely: if the painted-door test shows weak appetite, retention becomes the bet instead.

02 · Revenue
Build My Routine routes around the search page — where Nykaa sells sponsored placement.
The tension

Guided routines bypass the browse surface that earns ad and placement revenue. Win discovery, lose the income that funds it? A real cannibalization risk, not a footnote.

Where I land

Judge it on net contribution, not gross: one converted full-margin routine can outweigh the placement fee on a search that gets abandoned anyway — but only if it converts, which is why placement revenue is a hard guardrail. And routing around search needn't mean routing around the money: a brand can pay to be the default pick within a fit-ranked set, clearly labelled. The monetisation moves with the shopper.

03 · Trust
A "neutral agent" is the moat — but Nykaa sells its own private-label brands.
The tension

The thesis rests on a fit-first, brand-neutral recommender. Yet Nykaa is incentivised to push the labels it owns. The first question a sharp shopper — or interviewer — asks: why trust a conflicted agent?

Where I land

Govern the conflict, don't hide it. Rank by fit and evidence, never by margin — and prove it: keep the ranking model on a different objective function from merchandising, disclose when a pick is a Nykaa brand, let every pick be swapped, and track private-label share of recommendations as a watched metric. The moat only holds while the neutrality is real and visible; the moment routines read as a house-brand push, trust — and the whole thesis — collapses.

Act 14 — Validation, not a scoreboard

I won't project a lift for a feature that doesn't exist.

A teardown can't honestly claim "+1.5 pts conversion." So instead of inventing a scoreboard, here's how I'd actually find out if Build My Routine works — the cheapest test that could kill it, the one number that decides, and the second-order effects I'd refuse to trade away.

The hypothesis

The consider→purchase drop-off is choice overload, not weak intent. If a guided routine does the comparison work for a stated concern, more sessions should end in a multi-product cart. If conversion doesn't move once the routine is doing the choosing, then discovery was never the real problem — and that result is the most useful thing this work could tell me.

The cheapest test that could kill it, first

1
Painted door~days · no engine
Drop a "Build My Routine" entry point into discovery that leads to a short concern form and a waitlist. Proves demand exists before a line of engine code is written.
Kill signal: tap-through is negligible — shoppers want to browse, not be guided.
2
Concierge (Wizard-of-Oz)~weeks · humans, not ML
For one concern — dullness on oily skin — a small team hand-builds routines for a cohort behind the same UI. Proves the routine creates value, not just curiosity, with zero engine investment.
Kill signal: the cohort converts no better than the search-led control. The friction wasn't choice.
3
MVP A/B, one concern~a quarter · real engine
Only if 1 and 2 clear: ship the real engine for that single concern to a holdout-controlled slice. Proves it survives at scale — and against the guardrails below — before it touches the catalog.
Decision point: ship, iterate, or kill on the pre-set thresholds — no post-hoc rationalizing.
